\section{Introduction}

Partial differential equations (PDEs) are essential in modern modelling. They have applications in finance, physics, medicine, and biology, to name a few \cite{straussPartialDifferentialEquations2007,evansPartialDifferentialEquations2010}. The discretization of PDEs, which enables numerical solutions of such equations, can produce linear systems of equations with a large number of unknowns. For instance, in modern applications, it is not uncommon with billions of unknowns \cite{ohanaWellLargeScaleCollection2025}. It is therefore essential with efficient algorithms to handle large scale linear systems of equations.

In this thesis, we study a class of elliptic PDEs, which typically occur in time-independent problems. This class of PDEs show up in steady-state problems, stochastic processes, electrostatics, and the diffusion and flow of a quantity in a region of space \cite{pavliotisStochasticProcessesApplications2014, evansPartialDifferentialEquations2010,straussPartialDifferentialEquations2007}.

In particular, we study the inverse problem of the form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:problem}
    \min_{f} \left\| \mathcal{K} f - y \right\|_{L^2(\partial\Omega)}^2
    + \lambda^2 \left\| W f \right\|_{L^2(\Omega)}^2
\end{equation}
subject to
\begin{eqnarray}\label{eq:pde}
    \begin{aligned}
    -\nabla \cdot \sigma\nabla u + c u &= f \quad \text{in } \Omega, \\
    \frac{\partial u}{\partial n} &= 0 \quad \text{on } \partial\Omega.
    \end{aligned}
\end{eqnarray}
Here, $\mathcal{K}$ is the forward operator which maps the source $f$ to the boundary values $\mathcal{K}f = u|_{\partial\Omega}$, $\sigma$ is the diffusion coefficient, $c$ is the reaction coefficient, and $y$ is the observed boundary data. This problem has numerous applications, such as inverse imaging, crack determination, and electromagnetic brain mapping \cite{nielsenComputingIschemicRegions2013,elulGenesisEEG1971,PDFRecoveryCracks}. This problem is a source identification task: we are given observed data $y$, and seek to find the original source term $f$ such that $\mathcal K f = y$.

However, source identification problems of this form are ill-posed, as the operator $\mathcal K$ has a large null space. Consequently, solutions are not uniquely determined by the data. Moreover, a small perturbation to the observed data may result in large variations in the reconstructed source. As a result of these challenges, typical techniques such as Tikhonov regularization often fail to recover a meaningful source term.

Indeed, due to the rapidly decreasing singular values of the forward operator $\mathcal K$, Tikhonov reconstructions tend to appear on or near the boundary of the domain, corresponding to directions which appear near the null space of the operator. To address this issue, Elvetun and Nielsen proposed a weighted formulation which uses knowledge about the operator \cite{elvetunRegularizationOperatorSource2020}. The resulting weights $W$ are constructed in a way to penalize solutions which are located near the boundary. This method shows promising results, with reconstructions being located correctly, but with smoothing artifacts and a loss of the original source magnitude.

However, the construction of the weights $W$ is computationally expensive. Each column of $W$ involves solving \eqref{eq:pde} with a corresponding basis function as right-hand side. Thus, a naive implementation of the method requires one PDE solve per mesh node (or degree of freedom) in the discretization of $\Omega$. Hence, the computation cost grows with the resolution of the mesh. In two spatial dimensions, the number of mesh nodes, denoted $N$, increases quadratically with the mesh resolution, whereas in three dimensions it increases cubically. This makes the method computationally infeasible for fine discretizations or large domains.

To address this issue, we propose a matrix-free variant of the randomized SVD (rSVD). Standard rSVD uses random sampling to find an approximate rank-$k$ factorization $K_k = U\Sigma V^T$ of an explicitly formed matrix. Our approach applies $\mathcal{K}$ and $\mathcal{K}^*$ directly as operators on vectors, avoiding the construction of $\mathcal{K}$ entirely. The resulting factorization can then be used to construct the weights $W$ efficiently.

Due to the large null space of $\mathcal{K}$, and its rapid decay of singular values, the target rank $k$ can be selected much smaller than $N$. However, the matrix-free rSVD approach is not limited to problems where $\mathcal{K}$ has a large null space. Whenever $\mathcal{K}$ can be approximated well by a low-rank matrix, a rank-$k$ rSVD approximation captures the actions of the operator well. The suggested method is therefore not limited to the particular $\mathcal{K}$ studied in this thesis, but can be applied to a broader class of source identification problems.

The target rank $k$ also acts as a form of regularization. In practice, because the singular values of $\mathcal{K}$ decay rapidly, components that correspond to small singular values are often dominated by noise introduced by the environment and the equipment. By removing these components, the method acts as a rank-$k$ truncation, which improves the stability of the reconstructions.

In addition to the matrix-free rSVD method, we propose a dynamical low-rank scheme for the weighted Tikhonov problem. The scheme adapts the rank-adaptive dynamical low-rank method proposed by Schotthöfer et al. for neural network training \cite{schotthoferLowrankLotteryTickets2022} to the setting of source identification. We replace the gradient descent updates with conjugate gradient (CG) directions, and explore the use of preconditioners to accelerate the convergence.

As a result of the quadratic objective function from the weighted Tikhonov problem, our method shows improved convergence rate compared to a standard gradient descent implementation, with further improvement achieved using preconditioners. Additionally, since the source term $f$ is typically low-rank, our approach yields improved reconstructions compared to a full-rank approach.

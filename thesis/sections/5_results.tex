\subsection{Computational setup}
All numerical experiments were performed on a laptop running Fedora Linux 43 with Linux kernel version 6.19. The system was running with an Intel(R) Core(TM) i7-10510U (4 cores, 8 threads) with 16GB of memory.

Python 3.9 was used for all computations, together with NumPy and SciPy for linear algebra routines \cite{harrisArrayProgrammingNumPy2020,virtanenSciPy10Fundamental2020}. The FEniCS software was used for solving PDEs using the finite element method \cite{alnaesFEniCSProjectVersion2015,loggAutomatedSolutionDifferential2012}. All library specifications, algorithm implementations, and notebooks used to generate the results in this thesis are openly available at: \href{https://github.com/EliasSev/masters-thesis}{github.com/EliasSev/masters-thesis}.

\subsubsection{Computational time}\label{sec:computational_time}
\input{figures/rsvd_computational_time_comp.tex}
\input{tables/rsvd_computational_time_comp.tex}
The left plot of Figure~\ref{fig:rsvd_computational_time_comp} shows the computational time $t_{\text{rSVD}}$ of Algorithm~\ref{alg:discrete_rsvd} for target rank $k=10, 25, 50$, with oversampling parameter $p=5$, compared to the time $t_K$ to form $K = T A^{-1} M$ explicitly (Section~\ref{sec:approximating}) over increasingly fine meshes. 
The right plot omits $t_K$: at $N = 20{,}000$, the reference time is already $t_K = 339$ seconds (Table~\ref{tab:rsvd_computational_time_comp}), making explicit formation infeasible at larger scales. The median is taken over 30 runs. 
Table~\ref{tab:rsvd_computational_time_comp} shows the ratio $t_K / t_{\text{rSVD}}$, which is the speedup from using Algorithm~\ref{alg:discrete_rsvd} compared to forming $K$ explicitly (time reported for a single run).

The time $t_K$ surpasses $t_{\text{rSVD}}$ for $N=1184$, $1868$, and $2210$ for $k=10$, $25$, and $50$, respectively, with the time $t_K$ increasing quadratically and $t_{\text{rSVD}}$ increasing linearly with $N$. This is consistent with the theoretical costs of $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ for forming $K$ and $\mathcal{O}(Nl)$ for Algorithm~\ref{alg:discrete_rsvd}, established in Section~\ref{sec:approximating} \textcolor{red}{[TODO: mention this in Section~\ref{sec:approximating}]}.
When the mesh has $20{,}000$ nodes, the use of Algorithm~\ref{alg:discrete_rsvd} is between 185 and 436 times as fast, as reported in Table~\ref{tab:rsvd_computational_time_comp}.

The computational times $t_{\text{rSVD}}$ for target ranks $10$, $25$ and $50$ scale linearly, as seen in the right plot of Figure~\ref{fig:rsvd_computational_time_comp}. The slope of the linear increase depends on the target rank, with a higher target rank resulting in a steeper slope. For $N=500$, increasing $k$ from 10 to 50 results in an increase of $t_{\text{rSVD}}$ with a factor of 1.5, and for $N=300{,}000$, this factor grows to 2.3.

For the mesh sizes used in the remaining experiments \textcolor{red}{[TODO: which sections?]}, $N$ is approximately chosen between 1000 and 4000, making Algorithm~\ref{alg:discrete_rsvd} approximately $3$ to $30$ times faster than forming $K$ explicitly.


\subsubsection{Computational breakdown}
\input{figures/rsvd_computational_breakdown.tex}
\input{tables/rsvd_computational_breakdown.tex}
Figure~\ref{fig:rsvd_computational_breakdown} shows the median computational time of different stages of Algorithm~\ref{alg:discrete_rsvd} for selected $N$ and increasing $k$, with the relative computational cost shown in the bottom row. The median is taken over 30 runs for $N \le 40{,}000$ and 10 runs for $N = 400{,}000$. The `Setup' stage includes the assembly of the mass matrices $M$ and $M_\partial$, and the setup of the $K$ and $K^*$ solvers (Section~\ref{sec:approximating}) \textcolor{red}{[TODO: add this to Section~\ref{sec:approximating}]}. The other stages of Algorithm~\ref{alg:discrete_rsvd} are `2. $Y = K\Psi$' (lines 1--6), `3. $QR = Y$' (lines 7--8), `4. $B = Q^T K$' (lines 9--14), and `5. $(Q\tilde U)\Sigma V^T$' (lines 15--18). Table~\ref{tab:rsvd_computational_breakdown} shows relative computational time for selected target ranks and mesh sizes.

The main costs of Algorithm~\ref{alg:discrete_rsvd} are the forward solves using $K$ (stage 2) and the adjoint solves using $K^*$ (stage 4), both of which scale linearly with $k$.. The third largest cost is stage 5, which includes computing the SVD of $B \in \mathbb{R}^{l \times N}$, and computing $U = Q \tilde U \in \mathbb{R}^{N_b \times l}$, where $l = k + p$. The cost of the setup in stage 1 and the orthogonalization in stage 3 remain low compared to the other stages for all mesh sizes. The setup stage does not depend on $k$; its relative cost therefore decreases as $k$ increases. The QR factorization of the $N_b \times l$ matrix $Y$ accounts for less than 0.1\% of the total time.

The relative computational costs stabilize around $k = 100$ (all ranges below are for $N = 4{,}000$ to $N = 400{,}000$): setup accounts for 6\% to 3\%, stage 2 takes approximately 50\%, stage 4 takes 35\% to 26\%, and stage 5 takes 9\% to 22\%. The remaining fraction is the orthogonalization. At $k = 25$, stage 2 and stage 4 remain the dominant costs, but setup accounts for a larger share (14\% to 8\%), since its absolute time is unaffected by $k$, while stage 5 accounts for 3\% to 5\%.


\subsubsection{Target rank effect on reconstruction quality}
\input{figures/test_problems_selected.tex}
Figure~\ref{fig:test_problems_selected} shows the weighted Tikhonov solution of Equation~\eqref{eq:discrete_inverse_problem} for selected target ranks with oversampling parameter $p = 5$. The left panel shows the solution using the true $K$ and $W$, while the middle and right panels shows solutions Using $K$ and $W$ approximated by Algorithm~\ref{alg:discrete_rsvd}. The regularization parameter is set to $\lambda = 10^{-4}$ for all solutions.

In Figure~\ref{fig:test_problem_I}, using a target rank of $k=10$, the location of the source is correctly identified. The reconstruction exhibits more smoothing than the exact Tikhonov solution; the regularization already produces significant smoothing \cite{elvetunRegularizationOperatorSource2020}, and the low-rank approximation at $k=10$ increases this effect. Using $k=30$, as shown in the right panel, the source is essentially identical to the exact Tikhonov solution. For Problem II and Problem III of Figure~\ref{fig:test_problem_II} and \ref{fig:test_problem_III}, respectively, we see that the location is slightly incorrect for $k=10$, with the reconstructed source shifted toward the interior of the domain. Additionally, the reconstruction is more smoothed than the exact Tikhonov solution, as in Problem I. These issues are not present when choosing a larger target rank of $k=30$.

At $k = 30$, the rSVD recovers the exact Tikhonov solution across all three problems at a fraction of the computational cost (Section~\ref{sec:computational_time}).
At $k = 10$, the source location is recovered but the reconstruction is more heavily smoothed, and for multiple sources, the locations are shifted towards the interior of the domain.

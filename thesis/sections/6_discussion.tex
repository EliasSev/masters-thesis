\subsubsection{Asymptotic analysis}
To explain the computational times and asymptotic behavior in Sections~\ref{sec:computational_time} and \ref{sec:computational_breakdown}, we examine the cost of forming $K$ and of Algorithm~\ref{alg:discrete_rsvd} in more detail.

For a 2D elliptic problem, the Cholesky factorization of $A$ and $M_\partial$ can be done in $\mathcal{O}(N^{3/2})$ and $\mathcal{O}(N_b^{3/2})$ computations, respectively (Section~\ref{sec:initial_matrix_factorizations}). The resulting triangular systems can be solved at the respective costs $\mathcal{O}(N \log N)$ and $\mathcal{O}(N_b \log N_b)$. Since $N_b \ll N$, we may disregard the factorization and triangular solves using $M_\partial$. 

The cost of computing $K = T A^{-1} M$ involves solving $N_b$ linear systems $A v_i = T^T e_i$ (Section~\ref{sec:approximating}), each system costing $\mathcal{O}(N \log N)$ using a sparse Cholesky factorization of $A$. With $N_b \sim \sqrt{N}$, the cost therefore scales as $\mathcal{O}(N^{3/2} \log N)$, explaining the scaling of $t_K$ observed in the left panel of Figure~\ref{fig:rsvd_computational_time_comp}.

For the matrix-free rSVD (Algorithm~\ref{alg:discrete_rsvd}), factorizing $A$ costs $\mathcal{O}(N^{3/2})$, and is done once. Each application of $K$ and $K^*$ costs $\mathcal{O}(N \log N)$, for a total of $\mathcal{O}(l N \log N)$. The SVD of the $l \times N$ matrix $B$ costs $\mathcal{O}(l^2 N)$ \cite{golubMatrixComputations4th2013}, and the cost of the QR factorization of the small $N_b \times l$ matrix $Y$ is $\mathcal{O}(l^2 N_b)$ \cite{golubMatrixComputations4th2013}, and may be disregarded. Hence, assuming two dimensions and an efficient sparse Cholesky factorization of $A$, Algorithm~\ref{alg:discrete_rsvd} costs
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rsvd_cost}
    \underbrace{\mathcal{O}(N^{3/2})}_{\text{setup}}
    + \underbrace{\mathcal{O}(l N \log N)}_{\substack{\text{forward and} \\ \text{adjoint solves}}}
    + \underbrace{\mathcal{O}(l^2 N)}_{\text{SVD of } B}.
\end{equation}

This is consistent with the results in the right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:rsvd_computational_time_comp}: $t_{\text{rSVD}}$ scales near-linearly on the observed range, matching the near-linear scaling of $\mathcal{O}(N \log N)$. Even if $\mathcal{O}(N^{3/2})$ dominates $\mathcal{O}(N \log N)$ at large $N$, the hidden constant of the big-O notation makes the scaling be more similar to $\mathcal{O}(N \log N)$, which is confirmed by Figure~\ref{fig:rsvd_computational_breakdown}, where the relative cost of the setup stays constant for larger $N$.

Equation~\eqref{eq:rsvd_cost} also explains the cost breakdown in Figure~\ref{fig:rsvd_computational_breakdown}. For larger $k$, a larger share is taken up by the SVD stage, as predicted by $\mathcal{O}(l^2 N)$. For $k = 200$ ($l = k + p$), the largest considered target rank, we observed that as $N$ increases, the forward and adjoint solves start taking up a larger share of the time compared to the SVD of $B$, also consistent with Equation~\eqref{eq:rsvd_cost}. The cost $\mathcal{O}(l^2 N_b)$ of the QR-factorization $QR = Y$ is negligible, matching the results for all $k$ and $N$.

To understand the difference in the reconstruction quality, it is helpful to investigate the structure of $Y = K\Psi$ and $Y' = K^* \Psi'$, which form the sample matrix in Algorithm~\ref{alg:discrete_rsvd} and \ref{alg:discrete_rsvd_Kstar}, respectively. For the sake of simplicity, assume that $K^* = K$, and let $K = U \Sigma V^T$ be the SVD of $K$. The matrix $\tilde \Psi = V^T \Psi$ is a rotation of the random matrix $\Psi$, meaning that $\tilde \Psi$ also is a random matrix. Indeed, the columns of $Y$ are linear combinations of the left singular vectors $u_1, \dots, u_{N_b}$, since
\begin{equation*}
    Y = (U\Sigma)\tilde\Psi.
\end{equation*}
In a similar manner, the columns of $Y' = K^* \Psi' = (V \Sigma)\tilde\Psi'$ are linear combinations of the right singular values $v_1, \dots, v_{N_b}$.

The quality of the approximate SVD of the rSVD is entirely determined by the sampling $Y$ or $Y'$. Since $K$ is of rank $N_b$, we have that
\begin{equation*}
    \operatorname{Col}(K) =
    \operatorname{span}\{ u_1, \dots, u_{N_b} \},
    \quad
    \operatorname{Col}(K)^\perp = \{ 0 \},
\end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*}
    \operatorname{Col}(K^T) =
    \operatorname{span}\{v_1, \dots, v_{N_b}\},
    \quad
    \operatorname{Col}(K^T)^\perp =
    \operatorname{span}\{v_{N_b+1}, \dots, v_N\}.
\end{equation*}
